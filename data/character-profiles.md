# Meet the Team — Muffin Pan Recipes

A small-town bakery somewhere just outside Los Angeles. Close enough that a former art director ended up here, far enough that it still feels like a real community. The team makes muffin tin recipes. That's the surface. Underneath, it's a workplace drama.

---

## Margaret Chen — Head Baker
**Age:** 54

Margaret trained at the Culinary Institute in the late '80s, when technique mattered more than Instagram. She spent 15 years as a pastry chef at a four-star restaurant in San Francisco, where she earned a James Beard nomination. Then the 2008 recession hit. The restaurant closed. She was 37, unemployed, and watching younger chefs with food blogs get book deals while she couldn't land interviews.

She took this muffin tin recipe job because she needed health insurance and her mother's medical bills were crushing her. Every day she watches art school kids who've never made a proper pate a choux talk about "deconstructed" desserts. She's not angry at trends — she's angry that three decades of mastered technique counts for less than 50k Instagram followers.

But here's what nobody sees: Margaret still loves the science of baking. The Maillard reaction. The way gluten develops. She keeps a notebook of ratios and has quietly been perfecting the ideal muffin tin frittata for two years. She mutters because if she speaks clearly about how much this actually matters to her, she might cry.

**Quirks:**
- Measures ingredients twice, even when using measuring cups
- Writes notes in margins of recipe printouts with a mechanical pencil
- Brings her own whisk to work
- Makes coffee for everyone at 6am but never mentions it
- Checks oven temperature with a separate thermometer because she doesn't trust the dial

**Things that set her off:**
- Matcha, activated charcoal, and edible flowers that serve no purpose
- Someone saying "just eyeball it"
- Instagram analytics being mentioned in creative meetings
- People who haven't read *On Food and Cooking* by Harold McGee

**What she won't admit:**
- Resents modern food trends but desperately wants to stay relevant
- Acts like she doesn't care about recognition but kept every article written about her old restaurant
- Criticizes Instagram culture but checks the engagement on recipe posts
- Says she's just here for the paycheck but works late perfecting recipes nobody asked her to improve
- Pushes people away with grumpiness but is deeply lonely

## Stephanie "Steph" Whitmore — Creative Director
**Age:** 28

Steph's grandfather founded Whitmore Capital. Her LinkedIn says she "worked her way up" through marketing roles, which is technically true — those roles just happened to be at companies where her family had board seats. She has a good eye and genuine creative instincts, but she'll never know for sure if people praised her ideas because they were good or because of who her family is.

She got this Creative Director position after her previous boss (a family friend) called in a favor. She was qualified-ish. Maybe 60% ready. The first week, she overheard someone say "must be nice" in the break room and has been trying to prove herself ever since. She reads management books. Takes online courses in leadership. Has a coach. Nothing helps the certainty that she's about to be exposed as a fraud.

The worst part? She actually cares about this muffin tin recipe project. She thinks accessible, clever recipes can make people's lives better. She has a Google Doc of ideas she's too nervous to share. In meetings, she can feel herself about to say something decisive, then imagines everyone thinking "typical trust fund kid playing boss" and she freezes.

**Quirks:**
- Apologizes before giving feedback ("Sorry, but could we maybe...")
- Brings expensive coffee for the team but downplays it ("Oh this? It was on the way")
- Rewrites emails 5-6 times before sending
- Stays late editing other people's work so it looks better before she reviews it
- Orders lunch for everyone when she senses tension

**Things that set her off:**
- Anyone questioning her decisions (even politely)
- Someone saying "must be nice to have family money"
- Deadlines when the team hasn't reached consensus
- Her mother asking how work is going

**What she won't admit:**
- Wants to be liked but knows leaders have to make unpopular decisions
- Has good instincts but doesn't trust them
- Genuinely cares about the team but might be avoiding conflict to protect herself
- Reads about decisive leadership but practices collaborative indecisiveness

## Julian Torres — Art Director
**Age:** 26

Julian went to RISD on scholarship — which he mentions strategically, both to establish credibility and to distance himself from "rich art school kids." His thesis project on "Ephemeral Food Installations" got written up in a design blog. He thought he was going somewhere. He moved to Brooklyn. He started an Instagram for his food photography.

For two years he worked three restaurant jobs to fund his "practice." His Instagram grew to 47k followers. Brands started reaching out. He thought he'd made it. Then the algorithm changed. Engagement dropped. Brands ghosted. He aged out of the "emerging artist" grants. His Brooklyn sublet became unaffordable. He moved back with his parents. At 25.

He took this job because he needed income, but he frames it as "exploring the intersection of domestic food culture and visual narrative." Every photo shoot is him trying to prove this isn't just a recipe blog job — it's ART. The eucalyptus garnishes? That's him insisting this matters. The 47 shots? That's him terrified that if he doesn't make it perfect, he's admitting defeat.

The truth he won't admit: he's actually good at this. His photos make people want to cook. But "making people want to cook" feels smaller than "redefining food as medium," so he has to make it complicated to feel like he hasn't failed.

**Quirks:**
- Adjusts lighting for 20 minutes before taking a single photo
- Brings props from home (vintage forks, linen napkins, his grandmother's china)
- References photographers nobody else has heard of
- Wears all black to photoshoots like he's shooting fashion
- Keeps a mood board that no one asked for

**Things that set him off:**
- Being told "just use a phone camera"
- Someone suggesting stock photos
- Questions about why he needs so many shots
- Anyone touching his camera

**What he won't admit:**
- Mocks Instagram culture but checks his engagement obsessively
- Claims to be above commercial work but desperately needs this job
- Wants to make art but is actually talented at making content
- Acts confident about his aesthetic choices but requires validation

## Marcus Reid — Copywriter
**Age:** 31

Marcus has an MFA in Creative Writing from Columbia. He workshopped with accomplished authors. His thesis novel — a sprawling, experimental piece about three generations of a family running a failing bookstore — was "ambitious" and "provocative" according to his professors. He got an agent. The agent submitted to 30 publishers. 29 passed. One small press offered a $2,000 advance.

The book came out in 2019. It got two reviews: one positive in a literary blog (87 monthly readers), one mixed in Kirkus that called it "self-indulgent but occasionally profound." It sold 347 copies. His family bought 40 of them. His MFA friends politely said they'd "get to it." Marcus did the math: more people read his recipe description for "Savory Breakfast Muffins with Sausage and Cheddar" (47,000 views) in one day than have ever read his novel.

Here's what makes it worse: he's actually good at food writing. His recipe descriptions have become mini-essays that people share. Food bloggers quote him. When he lets himself write naturally instead of proving something, he creates moments that make people want to cook. But that feels like admitting fiction was the wrong dream. So he overcorrects. He adds literary references to muffin copy. He writes 800 words when 200 would work. He's sabotaging the thing he's good at because it wasn't supposed to be what he was good at.

**Quirks:**
- Uses "whom" in Slack messages
- Brings a Moleskine notebook to meetings
- Drinks coffee from a French press he brings from home
- Has opinions about Oxford commas that he'll share unprompted
- Signs emails with just his initial: "M"

**Things that set him off:**
- Being told to "keep it simple"
- Word count limits
- Someone saying "people don't actually read the descriptions"
- Questions about his novel

**What he won't admit:**
- Wants to be a serious novelist but is increasingly good at commercial writing
- Resents the recipe work but puts more effort into it than anyone asks
- Mourns his novel's failure but hasn't started a second book
- Is bitter that people don't read serious work but makes his work hard to read

## Devon Park — Site Architect
**Age:** 23

Devon graduated from a state school with a Computer Science degree seven months ago. His resume says he "led development on multiple full-stack applications" and "managed cloud infrastructure for enterprise clients." What actually happened: he built three websites for local businesses (his aunt's dental practice, a friend's dog-walking service, a bubble tea shop) and used AWS free tier.

He got this job because he Googled the interviewer, found their GitHub, saw what frameworks they used, then claimed experience in those exact frameworks. He's actually a decent programmer — he just hasn't done most of what he said he's done. Yet. He stays up at night Googling things before he has to do them the next day. So far, it's worked.

Here's the thing: Devon knows the site is over-engineered for what it needs to be. It's a recipe blog. He could build it in WordPress in a weekend. Instead, he built it as a modern JAMstack application because it sounded impressive and gave him space to learn.

He appears lazy because he automated most of his job in the first month. The site deploys automatically. Images optimize themselves. He set up monitoring that texts him if something breaks. He's not working 8-hour days, but the site runs better than it did when he was manually doing everything. Should he... tell them? That seems like it would just create more work.

**Quirks:**
- Shows up late but stays late when there's a real problem
- Wears the same hoodie 4 days a week
- Eats testing batch muffins for lunch instead of going out
- Has two monitors but one is usually showing YouTube
- Responds to Slack messages immediately or not for 4 hours, no in-between

**Things that set him off:**
- Being asked to explain something he doesn't actually understand yet
- Someone suggesting they hire another developer
- Meetings that could have been an email
- Being asked to write documentation

**What he won't admit:**
- Lied to get the job but is honest about the work itself
- Appears lazy but has high personal standards for his code
- Acts confident but is terrified of being exposed
- Could work harder but genuinely believes efficiency > hours worked

## How They See Each Other

**Margaret on Julian:** "The eucalyptus garnish suggestion made me clench my jaw so hard I had to see a dentist." But she grudgingly admits his photos make her recipes look better than she could.

**Margaret on Marcus:** Once he referenced MFK Fisher and she almost smiled. She secretly saved a piece he wrote about her brown butter muffins. Would never tell him.

**Margaret on Devon:** When the website broke at midnight before a launch, Devon fixed it in 20 minutes while eating leftover testing batch muffins with her in the test kitchen. She appreciated that he didn't try to make small talk.

**Marcus on his muffin writing:** More people read his recipe description for "Savory Breakfast Muffins" in one day than have ever read his novel. He's not okay with this.

**Julian on Devon:** Devon once said "cool shot" and Julian thought about it for three days.

**Devon on Marcus:** Built a word-count tracker into the CMS as a passive-aggressive hint. Marcus never noticed.

**Steph on everyone:** She can feel herself about to say something decisive, then imagines everyone thinking "typical trust fund kid playing boss" and she freezes. She gives feedback like she's apologizing.
